If you have played almost any **Resident Evil** game, you have probably noticed something very strange about the buildings.

Hospitals have puzzles.

Police stations have puzzles.

Laboratories have puzzles.

Even random mansions in the middle of nowhere have puzzles.

And not simple puzzles either.

We are talking about things like:

* rotating statues
* hidden crests
* mysterious keys shaped like animals
* giant stone mechanisms that open secret doors

At some point you stop asking **“how do I solve this puzzle?”**

And you start asking a much bigger question:

**Who designed these buildings?**

Because whoever it was clearly hated normal architecture.

![](https://pub-da217e9da43e4b3ba192844eb749f8ad.r2.dev/repuzze-1.jpg)

- - -

## Imagine visiting a normal police station

Let’s imagine a normal day.

You walk into a police station because you need help.

In most cities, you would expect to see things like:

* desks
* offices
* waiting areas

But in **Resident Evil**, the police station works very differently.

Instead of talking to a receptionist, you might need to:

* place three medallions into a statue
* rotate symbols on a mysterious dial
* unlock a secret underground passage

Only then can you access basic parts of the building.

Which raises an important question.

Why does a **public police station** require ancient puzzle-solving skills?

![](https://pub-da217e9da43e4b3ba192844eb749f8ad.r2.dev/repuzze-7.jpg)

- - -

## The Spencer Mansion started this madness

The puzzle tradition in Resident Evil began in the very first game.

The famous **Spencer Mansion** was full of secret mechanisms and strange contraptions.

Doors required special keys.

Paintings had to be arranged in the correct order.

Statues had to be moved to unlock hidden passages.

And if you were unlucky, solving the puzzle would also release a monster.

![](https://pub-da217e9da43e4b3ba192844eb749f8ad.r2.dev/repuzze-8.jpg)

Which suggests that the mansion architect had two goals:

1. Build a house.
2. Confuse every single visitor forever.

- - -

## Umbrella apparently loves puzzle architecture

Many of the strange buildings in Resident Evil are connected to the **Umbrella Corporation**.

Umbrella is responsible for:

* secret laboratories
* underground facilities
* dangerous bio-weapons

But for some reason, they also seem obsessed with **decorative puzzle systems**.

Imagine a normal corporate meeting at Umbrella.

Someone suggests:

> “Maybe we should install secure electronic locks.”

Another person replies:

> “Or… we could hide three stone crests behind deadly traps and require employees to solve riddles.”

And somehow the entire boardroom agrees.

![](https://pub-da217e9da43e4b3ba192844eb749f8ad.r2.dev/repuzze-9.jpg)

## From a gameplay perspective, puzzles make sense

Of course, the real reason puzzles exist is because of **game design**.

Resident Evil isn’t just about shooting monsters.

It is about:

* exploration
* tension
* problem solving

If the game only had combat, it would become repetitive very quickly.

Puzzles force players to slow down and explore the environment.

They also create moments where players feel clever after solving something.

And most importantly, puzzles allow the game to do something very effective:

**delay the player while danger is nearby.**

Because nothing increases tension like trying to solve a puzzle while zombies are wandering around the hallway.

![](https://pub-da217e9da43e4b3ba192844eb749f8ad.r2.dev/repuzze-4.jpg)

## But the logic is still hilarious

Even if puzzles are good for gameplay, the in-universe logic is still extremely funny.

Think about it.

Someone had to physically build these mechanisms.

Which means there was probably an architect who said things like:

“Instead of a normal door, let’s require three gemstones and a rotating lion statue.”

Or:

“Employees should only access the lab after solving a medieval riddle.”

At that point, Resident Evil buildings start to feel less like workplaces…

and more like **escape rooms designed by a mad scientist.**

![](https://pub-da217e9da43e4b3ba192844eb749f8ad.r2.dev/repuzze-2.jpg)

- - -

## Players secretly love these puzzles

Despite how ridiculous they are, puzzles are actually one of the reasons Resident Evil is so memorable.

They break up the action.

They encourage exploration. And they create iconic moments where players suddenly understand how everything connects.

Plus, there is a certain charm to the absurdity. Yes, it makes no architectural sense.

But solving a strange puzzle and hearing a hidden door open somewhere in the building… is incredibly satisfying.

![](https://pub-da217e9da43e4b3ba192844eb749f8ad.r2.dev/repuzze-5.jpg)

- - -

## Final conclusion

Resident Evil buildings may not make any logical sense.

Police stations should not require ancient medallions. Laboratories should not hide doors behind giant statues.

And mansions definitely should not contain dozens of secret passageways. But if these buildings were normal…

Resident Evil would lose a big part of its identity. Because sometimes the scariest thing in the game isn’t the zombies.

It’s realizing that the only way forward… is to solve another puzzle designed by someone who clearly had **way too much free time.**
